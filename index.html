## Atmospheric Reality Gap

Your homepage headline reads "Welcome to Mount Olympus Brewing" — a name that carries mythological weight, craft ambition, and regional pride. Your hero image is filed as `IMG_9436.jpg` and its alt text describes it plainly as a "modern commercial building with large windows and landscaped front yard with small trees and rocks." That is not a brand moment. That is a real estate listing. The exterior-building-as-hero is one of the weakest possible visual choices for a craft brewery because it communicates address, not experience. A buyer deciding between you and a competitor who leads with a pint catching afternoon light, a taproom full of people who look like they belong there, or a brewer mid-process has already made a subconscious decision before they read a single word. You have four images on the homepage total — none of which I can see, but the filenames `IMG_9436.jpg` and `IMG_9710.jpg` confirm they were never renamed, never art-directed for web deployment, and almost certainly were not shot for conversion. The trust cost: visitors arriving at #1 on Google find a brand that named itself after a god and photographs itself like a strip mall.

## Visual Narrative Coherence

Two of your 16 homepage links are broken. The first is `https://mountolympusbrewing.com/wholesale` returning a password protected page — meaning a wholesale buyer, a bar owner, a grocery buyer clicking that navigation link hits an authentication wall with no explanation and no redirect. That buyer does not wait. They close the tab. The second broken link is your Facebook page at `http://www.facebook.com/mountolympusbrewing` returning a 400, which means your own navigation is sending social-curious visitors to a dead destination. A high-value buyer — say, a regional distributor checking you out before a meeting — is testing your operational competence through your website. Two broken links in 16 is a 12.5% failure rate on the homepage alone. That is not a minor technical debt. That is evidence of an unmaintained brand.

Your navigation also reveals a structural problem: you maintain separate pages for Aberdeen and Tumwater but surface both from the same homepage with no clear visual or copy distinction about what makes each location worth a visit. The visual assets, if they exist, are not doing the location-differentiation work. Buyers in Aberdeen and Tumwater need different reasons to care.

## Metadata and Conversion Gaps

Your page title is "Mount Olympus Brewing" at 21 characters — you are surrendering roughly 40 characters of indexable, intent-matching copy that could tell Google and the searcher what you are, where you are, and why you are worth clicking. Your meta description at 120 characters is present but the scan cannot confirm whether it communicates a reason to visit over a competitor. Your image filenames — `IMG_9436.jpg`, `IMG_9710.jpg` — are invisible to image search. Google Image Search cannot categorize, rank, or surface a file named after a camera counter. Craft brewery image search drives real local traffic. You are opted out of it by default. Your `og:image` tag is present, which means when someone shares your URL the preview image fires — but if that image is the building exterior shot from an unedited camera roll, every social share reinforces the wrong first impression at scale.

You rank #1 for your own name. That is the floor, not the ceiling. It means people who already know you can find you. It does not mean people choosing a brewery find you.
